I

What started this

The explorer Albert Lin spent years searching for lost tombs and cities not with a shovel, but with satellites — finding what was hidden by reading the surface of the Earth instead of digging blindly into it. That single idea is the seed of this project: that you need not stand on the ground, or even on the right continent, to find the traces of the people who came before.

For all its fame, the land around Dholavira — a 4,500-year-old Harappan city in the Rann of Kutch — remains barely surveyed. The excavated city is only its heart. What surrounds it — the outer settlements, the field systems, the water works that made desert life possible — lies largely unmapped, waiting. This project asks one quiet question: can free satellite data tell us where to look next?

II

What it sets out to do

i

Detect

Read free Sentinel-2 imagery for vegetation, moisture and soil — and mark the places that stand apart from everything around them.

ii

Verify

Rule out the ordinary — farms, salt, roads, geology — and keep only what endures across years and agrees across signals.

iii

Map

Lay the survivors on an honest map, each marked plainly as a possibility for the spade to test — never as a discovery already made.

iv

Grow

Reach outward, region by region — beginning here in Gujarat, built to travel anywhere the land still holds a story worth reading.

Statistics lift the truly unusual from the ordinary noise

Each candidate is found by measuring how far a place departs from its own surroundings — more than two standard deviations from the local normal — then filtered against land cover to rule out farmland, water and built-up ground, and weighed against every ordinary explanation. The method follows the approach professional satellite archaeology uses; the honesty about its limits is what earns it trust.
